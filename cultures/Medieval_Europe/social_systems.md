# Social Systems of Medieval Europe

## Government
### Feudal System
- Vassalage and homage ceremonies
- Land tenure in exchange for military service
- Complex web of overlapping loyalties
- Feudal courts and administrative procedures

### Monarchy
- Divine right of kings
- Coronation ceremonies and sacred kingship
- Royal courts and administrative systems
- Various forms: elected vs. hereditary monarchy

### Holy Roman Empire
- Complex elective monarchy
- Relationship between Emperor and Pope
- Administration of diverse territories
- Imperial Diet (Reichstag) for governance

### City-States and Republics
- Italian city-states (Venice, Florence, Genoa)
- Merchant oligarchies and republican government
- Civic constitutions and municipal law
- Urban autonomy from feudal obligations

## Economics
### Manorial System
- Agricultural estates (manors) with serfs
- Three-field crop rotation system
- Agricultural innovations (heavy plow, horse collar)
- Self-sufficient economic units

### Trade and Commerce
- Revival of long-distance trade (11th-13th centuries)
- Merchant guilds and craft guilds
- Banking systems (especially Italian)
- Champagne fairs and trade routes
- Urban commercial centers and markets

### Guild System
- Craft specialization and quality control
- Training systems (apprentice, journeyman, master)
- Economic regulation and pricing
- Social support systems for members

## Family Structure
### Christian Family Model
- Monogamous marriage as ideal
- Church's role in marriage regulations
- Extended family networks and kinship
- Lineage and genealogical importance

### Inheritance
- Primogeniture (eldest son inherits) in most contexts
- Partible inheritance in some regions
- Dowry and dower systems
- Strategies for managing family wealth and status

## Gender Roles
### Women's Roles
- **Noble women**: Estate management, education of children, religious patronage
- **Peasant women**: Agricultural work, household management, child-rearing
- **Urban women**: Craft production, trade in some contexts, guild participation
- **Religious women**: Convents, monasteries, religious life as alternative to marriage
- **Legal restrictions**: Limited property rights, male guardianship

### Men's Roles
- **Noble men**: Military service, estate management, vassalage
- **Peasant men**: Agricultural work, military service if called
- **Urban men**: Craft production, trade, guild membership
- **Religious men**: Clerical service, monastic life, education

## Education System
### Cathedral and Monastic Schools
- Education for clergy and future church officials
- Seven liberal arts (trivium and quadrivium)
- Classical texts preserved and studied
- Training in Latin and religious studies

### Universities
- Emergence of universities (11th-12th centuries)
- Specialized education in law, medicine, theology
- Scholastic method of learning
- International scholarly community

### Guild Training
- Apprenticeship systems for crafts
- Practical education in trades
- Journeyman years and mastery requirements
- Transmission of practical knowledge and skills

## Religious System
### Catholic Church
- Central authority with Pope as spiritual leader
- Hierarchical structure (pope, cardinals, bishops, priests)
- Sacramental system and religious practice
- Canon law and church-state relations

### Monasticism
- Benedictine, Cistercian, and other orders
- Preservation of learning and manuscripts
- Agricultural and economic contributions
- Spiritual models and religious authority

### Lay Piety
- Parish system and local religious practice
- Pilgrimage traditions
- Lay religious movements and devotions
- Popular religious practices and festivals

## Social Classes
- **Nobility**: Military elite, landowners, hereditary status
- **Clergy**: Religious leaders with special status, education, and authority
- **Urban Elite**: Merchants, craft masters, town officials
- **Peasantry**: Serfs (legally bound to land) and free peasants
- **Outcasts**: Various categories outside the three-estate system
