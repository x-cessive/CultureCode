# Social Systems of Mali Empire

## Government
### Imperial System
- Mansa as absolute ruler with divine authority
- Complex administrative system with court officials
- Provincial governors (farbas) managing regions
- Local chiefs maintaining traditional authority under empire

### Manden Charter
- Constitutional framework for the empire
- Rights and responsibilities of different social groups
- Principles of governance and justice
- Social contract between rulers and people

### Administrative Structure
- Bamanan (court officials) as imperial administrators
- Regional governors maintaining local order
- Military commanders protecting trade routes and borders
- Specialized officials for tax collection and justice

## Economics
### Trade Networks
- Control of trans-Saharan trade routes
- Gold mining and trade (primary source of wealth)
- Salt trade from Sahara mines
- Commerce in kola nuts, ivory, and enslaved people
- Trade centers like Timbuktu and Gao

### Currency and Banking
- Gold dust as primary currency
- Sophisticated weighing and testing systems
- Credit systems for long-distance trade
- Banking practices among merchant communities

### Agriculture
- Farming in fertile Niger River valley
- Crops: millet, sorghum, rice, cotton
- Irrigation and water management systems
- Livestock herding in savanna regions

## Family Structure
### Extended Family System
- Multiple generations living together
- Patriarchal system with elder authority
- Polygamy common among wealthy families
- Complex inheritance systems

### Social Organization
- Kinship groups (clans) as basic social units
- Age-grade systems for social organization
- Caste-like occupational groups (nyamakala)
- Distinction between free people and enslaved individuals

## Gender Roles
### Women's Roles
- **Royal women**: Significant political influence, especially queen mothers
- **Merchant women**: Active in trade and commerce
- **Farmers and artisans**: Primary agricultural labor and craft production
- **Religious roles**: Participation in Islamic and traditional religious practices
- **Legal status**: Some property rights, particularly in urban areas

### Men's Roles
- **Rulers and administrators**: Political and military leadership
- **Merchants and traders**: International and domestic commerce
- **Farmers and herders**: Agricultural and pastoral production
- **Religious leaders**: Islamic scholarship and traditional religious practice

## Education System
### Islamic Education
- Quranic schools in major cities
- Universities like Sankore in Timbuktu
- Islamic legal and theological education
- Scholarship in mathematics, astronomy, and medicine

### Traditional Education
- Oral tradition preservation by griots
- Training in royal courts and noble households
- Craft and trade apprenticeships
- Ritual and ceremonial education

### Griot Tradition
- Professional storytellers and historians
- Keepers of genealogies and oral histories
- Musical and artistic education
- Diplomatic and ceremonial roles

## Religious System
### Islam
- Official state religion under Mansa Musa and successors
- Integration with traditional beliefs
- Islamic law (Sharia) in urban centers
- Religious tolerance for traditional practices

### Traditional African Religion
- Continued practice of ancestral veneration
- Local deities and spiritual practices
- Ritual specialists and healers
- Syncretic practices combining Islamic and traditional beliefs

### Religious Centers
- Timbuktu as major center of Islamic learning
- Mosques throughout the empire
- Centers of Islamic scholarship and manuscript production
- Integration of religious and commercial activities

## Social Classes
- **Mansa (Emperor)**: Supreme political and religious authority
- **Royal Family**: Extended family with special privileges and roles
- **Court Officials (Bamanan)**: Administrative and military leaders
- **Free Commoners**: Farmers, herders, and ordinary citizens
- **Occupational Groups (Nyamakala)**: Professional musicians, artisans, etc.
- **Enslaved People**: Various categories with different statuses and rights
- **Religious Scholars**: Islamic clerics and teachers
- **Merchants**: International traders with special status
