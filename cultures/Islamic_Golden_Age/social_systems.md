# Social Systems of Islamic Golden Age

## Government
### Caliphate System
- Abbasid Caliphate as central authority (750-1258 CE)
- Caliph as both religious and political leader
- Administrative system with viziers and various departments
- Delegation of regional authority to governors (walis)

### Regional Governance
- Various sultanates and emirates with significant autonomy
- Military commanders (amirs) in different regions
- Local governance that often incorporated existing systems
- Administrative positions often filled by Persian and other non-Arab officials

### Legal System
- Islamic law (Sharia) as foundation
- Multiple schools of jurisprudence (madhabs)
- Qadis (judges) administering justice
- Integration of pre-Islamic legal practices where compatible

## Economics
### Trade Networks
- Extensive trade routes connecting Europe, Africa, and Asia
- Cities as commercial hubs (Baghdad, Cairo, Cordoba)
- Banking and credit systems (hawala)
- Standardized currency systems
- Protection of merchants and trade routes

### Agriculture
- Advanced irrigation systems
- Introduction of new crops from different regions
- Agricultural manuals and techniques
- Estate systems and various land tenure arrangements

### Manufacturing and Industry
- Textile production (silk, cotton)
- Paper manufacturing (introduced from China)
- Metalworking and crafts
- Glass and ceramic production
- Sugar refining technology

## Family Structure
### Islamic Family Model
- Extended family structures
- Patriarchal system with defined rights and duties
- Marriage contracts with specified terms
- Inheritance laws based on Quranic principles
- Polygamy permitted but with restrictions

### Social Mobility
- Possible through religious scholarship, trade, or military service
- Slaves could rise to high positions (especially military slaves - mamluks)
- Professional classes based on skills and knowledge
- Merit-based advancement in some administrative contexts

## Gender Roles
### Women's Rights
- Property ownership rights
- Inheritance rights (though often less than men's)
- Rights in marriage contracts
- Participation in commerce and trade
- Access to education and religious learning in some contexts

### Men's Roles
- Primary economic providers
- Political and military leadership
- Religious authority in most contexts
- Educational and scholarly pursuits

## Education System
### Institutions
- Madrasas (educational institutions)
- Libraries and centers of learning
- House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) in Baghdad
- Translation movements preserving and expanding knowledge

### Curriculum
- Religious studies (Quran, Hadith, jurisprudence)
- Sciences (mathematics, astronomy, medicine)
- Philosophy and logic
- Literature and languages
- Practical subjects (medicine, engineering)

### Scholarly Culture
- Travel for education (student mobility)
- Master-apprentice relationships
- Peer review of scholarly work
- International scholarly networks

## Religious System
### Islam as Unifying Force
- Religious tolerance for "People of the Book" (Christians, Jews)
- Dhimmi system providing protection for non-Muslims
- Integration of diverse religious communities in intellectual work
- Synthesis of Greek philosophy with Islamic theology

### Religious Practices
- Daily prayers, fasting, pilgrimage
- Religious education and instruction
- Sufi mystical traditions
- Religious festivals and ceremonies

## Social Classes
- **Ruling Elite**: Caliphs, sultans, viziers, military commanders
- **Religious Scholars (Ulama)**: Religious and legal authority
- **Military Class**: Professional soldiers and guards (including mamluks)
- **Merchants and Artisans**: Economic class with significant status
- **Peasants and Workers**: Agricultural laborers and common workers
- **Slaves**: Various categories with different legal and social statuses
