# Social Systems of Ancient Rome

## Government
Ancient Rome went through three major governmental systems:

### Monarchy (753-509 BCE)
- Seven legendary kings
- Senate as advisory body
- Curiate Assembly for lawmaking

### Republic (509-27 BCE)
- Dual consulship (two chief executives elected annually)
- Senate as primary legislative and advisory body
- Various assemblies representing different social classes
- Complex system of checks and balances
- Magistrates with specific powers (praetors, quaestors, aediles, tribunes)

### Empire (27 BCE-476 CE in West)
- Emperor as supreme authority
- Senate reduced to advisory role
- Bureaucratic system with appointed officials
- Provinces governed by appointed governors
- Military loyalty to emperor

## Economics
The Roman economy was diverse and expansive:

### Agriculture
- Large estates (latifundia) worked by slaves
- Production of grain, wine, olive oil
- Trade throughout the Mediterranean world

### Trade and Commerce
- Extensive trade networks across the empire
- Standardized currency system
- Roman roads facilitating commerce
- Ports connecting different regions

### Industry
- Mining operations (gold, silver, iron)
- Manufacturing (pottery, textiles, tools)
- Construction industry for public works

## Family Structure
- Patriarchal system with paterfamilias as absolute head
- Extended family units (familia)
- Adoption used for political and social purposes
- Clear distinction between patricians and plebeians initially
- Marriage customs varied over time

## Gender Roles
- **Men**: Citizens with political rights (in Republican era), heads of households, military service
- **Women**: Gradually gained more rights over time; could own property, especially in the Empire
- **Patrician women**: More restrictions in early Republic, greater freedom in later periods
- **Plebeian women**: Generally had more economic freedom in daily life

## Education System
- **Elementary education**: Reading, writing, basic arithmetic
- **Secondary education**: Grammar and literature (often Greek)
- **Higher education**: Rhetoric and oratory (for political careers)
- **Higher learning**: Philosophy and law (often in Greece)

## Military System
- Professional army under Empire (previously citizen-soldiers in Republic)
- Legionary system with standardized organization
- Auxiliary forces from non-citizen populations
- Military as path to citizenship for non-Romans
- Extensive fortifications and engineering projects

## Legal System
- Twelve Tables (451 BCE) as foundation of Roman law
- Praetorian law adapting to changing circumstances
- Citizen vs. non-citizen legal protections
- Gradual extension of citizenship throughout the empire

## Social Classes
- **Senatorial class**: Wealthy landowners eligible for Senate
- **Equestrian class**: Wealthy business class
- **Plebeians**: Common citizens (free-born Romans)
- **Slaves**: Property that could be freed (liberti)
- **Foreigners (peregrini)**: Non-citizens with various legal statuses
